# 3D Poster Card Perspective Reveal

## Primary Use Case

A premium cinematic hero-card animation used to introduce a key character, founder, product figure, iconic portrait, or symbolic centerpiece in a visually authoritative way.

This animation is designed for moments where the subject must feel important before any supporting text or surrounding UI takes over.

It works especially well for editorial hero sections, premium SaaS intros, creator branding sequences, portfolio landing sections, and high-trust product showcases.

The objective is not simply to display a card.

The objective is to make the subject feel elevated.

## Visual Behavior

A vertical premium poster card begins in a slightly rotated 3D perspective position.

The main subject remains visually centered and stable while the card itself handles the primary motion language.

The sequence creates a feeling of intentional arrival:

1. the card starts angled in perspective with subtle blur and depth
2. the composition rotates into a fully front-facing hero moment
3. the center asset resolves cleanly into focus
4. the layout holds briefly to create authority
5. the entire card glides into the lower-left quadrant
6. subtle idle floating keeps the composition alive afterward

The result should feel like product trailer motion rather than standard UI animation.

Movement must remain restrained and expensive.

---

## Motion Sequence

### Phase 1 — Perspective Entry

- card begins with rotateY and rotateX offset
- slight scale reduction
- slight blur present
- opacity reduced
- subject slightly softened with blur resolve

This creates anticipation and dimensionality.

---

### Phase 2 — Front-Facing Hero Lock

- card rotates to full front-facing alignment
- scale settles to full size
- blur resolves
- subject becomes sharp and fully visible
- vertical side typography subtly enters

This is the strongest visual moment.

This frame should feel like the hero frame.

---

### Phase 3 — Composition Shift

- card transitions smoothly into the lower-left quadrant
- slight scale reduction maintains hierarchy
- restrained rotation helps preserve elegance

This prepares space for future supporting text, CTA blocks, or adjacent content.

This is not an exit.

It is a compositional repositioning.

---

### Phase 4 — Ambient Idle Motion

- subtle floating motion
- minor vertical drift
- restrained shadow breathing
- minimal subject drift

The composition remains alive without becoming distracting.

Idle motion should never dominate.

## Avoid Using When

Avoid using this animation when:

- the section requires fast utility-first UI
- the subject is not visually strong enough to carry focus
- the design needs playful or highly energetic motion
- heavy text density must dominate immediately
- the page requires lightweight minimalist transitions only
- too many competing animated elements already exist
- the asset quality is weak or poorly cut out

This animation requires a strong centerpiece.

Weak assets break the illusion.

## Agent Notes

Use this pattern when the primary objective is to establish authority through a single centerpiece visual.

This animation is stronger than standard slide-ins because it creates emotional framing through perspective and controlled compositional movement.

The front-facing hold is critical.

Do not rush it.

The lower-left repositioning should feel like layout choreography, not disappearance.

Avoid excessive rotation.

Avoid loud glow.

Avoid aggressive scaling.

The asset should feel anchored while the card performs the motion.

The composition should feel like a premium Apple-style product trailer frame rather than a generic animated card.

This animation belongs in the intersection of:

- portrait intros
- editorial posters
- cinematic reveals
- premium hero systems
- authority-focused creator visuals

Use when the visual must feel expensive before the user reads a single word.
